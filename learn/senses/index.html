Use All of Your Senses

Many have asked us, "What is the best way to learn a language?" The answer we typically give them – immersion, at which point the smile fades from their faces. It’s a little disheartening seeing how some people give up so easily when they find something difficult; rather than making a plan to achieve, or at least approximate, what they perceive to be “impossible”. Instead, they resign themselves to do nothing or continue with their previous methods. Obviously not everyone can go to another country and immerse themselves in the language, but that doesn’t preclude them from faking it. Which brings me to the current topic – Faking Immersion.

So what is immersion anyway? Imagine being immersed in a pool of grape juice – the longer you are there, the more difficult it is to distinguish you from the grape juice. First, you’ll start smelling and tasting like grape juice; soak for a bit longer and you will even start to be the same color; stay too long, you’ll become the grape juice itself (not suggested)!

When learning a language, you can approximate immersion by using as many of your senses as possible. Seeing and hearing the language are probably obvious, buy you might raise your eyebrows at the thought of feeling, tasting, and smelling a language. What many people forget is that languages are cultural entities – if you try to separate the language from the culture, too much will be lost in the translation. What better way to understand culture than by tasting the food, smelling the vegetation, and feeling the music and terrain over which the language has developed and flourished?
