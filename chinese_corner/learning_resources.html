Learning Resources

Apps

Mango Languages
Great for Listening, Reading

Mango Languages is currently my favorite general language app. With real speakers, interactive script (Chinese characters with pinyin on hover), and lessons presented as conversation that is built upon previous vocabulary, this app is a great resource for learning. There are roughly 500 lessons, as well as some supplementary Mandarin courses discussing fengshui and the Chinese zodiac. I assume this program is typically expensive, but you can access it through almost any local library for free (make an account for the online resources at your library and sign in).

Pimsleur
Great for Listening

Pimsleur is an older program that is 100% audio, which makes it great for commutes or times when you want to let your eyes rest. Unlike many apps that simply “teach” vocabulary, Pimsleur lessons are presented as a conversation that builds upon previous vocabulary in 150 30-minute daily sessions. While the program can be fairly expensive, you can usually find the audio CDs at the library and in some cases, even the app offered through the online resources.

Note: I would suggest using this only after you have a strong understanding of the tones and sounds possible in Mandarin and as a supplement.

Duolingo
Great for Writing, Reading

While definitely not my first choice as a language learning app, the idea of gamified learning provides some with a powerful incentive. The one key advantage of this program is that you can get some typing practice and multiple answers are accepted (typically for typing practice, your answer has to be exactly the same, even though there are multiple ways to write a given sentence). Furthermore, Duolingo is crowdsourced, so when you aren’t sure about an answer, you can usually find a good explanation in the comments for that question.

Note: While there is audio, it is computerized and should only be used as a supplement.

Manga Mandarin
Great for Reading, Listening

This is one of my favorite reading apps. It presents short stories in the form of Manga, with pictures, interactive dialogue (click for pronunciation and tips), and grammar lessons as you are learning. Stories are ordered by HSK level (starting with HSK 0, i.e. absolute beginner), and range a variety of genera, such as action, mystery, horror, romance, etc. This app is semi-free – you can get more “beans” (their currency) by answering questions of the day in Chinese, completing tests, inviting friends, or paying.

Note: The audio is natural, but often a bit fast and full of colloquialisms, so it will help you learn “real” Chinese (i.e. what you will hear when you will visit).

DuShu
Great for Reading

Not a very flashy app, but I have found it well organized and great for learning how to read in Chinese. Many words in Chinese consist of two characters, and it isn’t always obvious how words with the same characters are related. In this app, not only can you see the meaning of a word (often two characters), you can also see the meaning of the individual characters. For me, this has been extremely useful for thinking about the characters and how they have combined.
