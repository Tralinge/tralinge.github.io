A Brief History of the English Language

Old English

Old English developed from Germanic dialects brought to Britain by the Anglo-Saxons as Roman rule of the island dwindled. The Germanic language of the Anglo-Saxons quickly became the dominant language, replacing Latin (brought by the Romans) and Celtic languages.

Several dialects of Old English emerged, but the West Saxon dialect grew to prominence with the rise of the kingdom of Wessex and became the standard for writing. During that period, writing shifted from runic script to Latin script. Old English is much more similar to German than Modern English. Here is an example from Beowulf:

Middle English

The 8th and 9th centuries saw waves of Norse colonization, causing Old English to be heavily influenced by Old Norse (a Northern Germanic Language), especially in the north.

In 1066, William the Conqueror invaded England and became the first Norman King of England. This led Old English to be heavily influenced by Old Norman (a Romance language related to French) as it was the language of the elites. Many words for politics and prestige are therefore closely related to French. During this period, English became more simplified and rigid, likely due to different systems of grammar. Here is an example from the Canterbury Tales:

Wepyng and waylyng, care and oother sorwe
I knowe ynogh, on even and a-morwe,'
Quod the Marchant, 'and so doon oother mo
That wedded been.

Weeping and wailing, care and other sorrow
I know enough, in the evening and in the morning,'
said the Merchant, 'and so do many others
who have been married.

Early Modern English

">In the 15th century, led by King Henry V, English started overtaking Norman French in prestige and was used in many official documents in Chancery Standard. Around the same time, the printing press was introduced to England, thereby increasing the prominence of Chancery Standard. Important works from that period include Shakespeare’s works and the King James bible.

Proliferation of Modern English

In the 18th century, Modern English spread through conquest by the British Empire and emerged as the de facto language of commerce, diplomacy, science, education, etc. Following independence of many of the former British colonies, several different dialects emerged through contact with local languages. With the rise of the United States as a superpower in the 20th century and proliferation of mass media, English became a truly global language, used more widely than any other language.
