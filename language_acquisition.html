Faking Immersion

As an ESL teacher for the last several years, I have met a diverse group of students, from South America to the Middle East and Asia. For me, languages has always been a bit of a passion, which is why I have learned to converse in various languages and at least get my point across even with limited knowledge of a language. After my students learn that I can speak a few different languages they never cease to ask the question “what is the secret to learning languages?” The answer I typically give them – immersion, at which point the smile fades from their face. It’s a little disheartening seeing how some people give up so easily when they find something difficult; rather than making a plan to achieve, or at least approximate, what they perceive to be “impossible”, they instead resign themselves to do nothing or continue with their previous method. Obviously not everyone can go to another country and immerse themselves in the language, but that doesn’t preclude them from faking it. Which brings me to the topic for today – Faking Immersion.

Pronunciation

So what is immersion anyway? Imagine being immersed in a pool of grape juice – the longer you are there, the more difficult it is to distinguish you from the grape juice. First, you’ll start smelling and tasting like grape juice; soak for a bit longer and you will even start to be the same color; stay too long, you’ll become the grape juice (not suggested).

When learning a language, you can approximate immersion by using as many of your senses as possible. Seeing and hearing the language are probably obvious, buy you might raise your eyebrows at the thought of feeling, tasting, and smelling a language. What many people forget is that languages are cultural entities – if you try to separate the language from the culture, too much will be lost in the translation. What better way to understand culture than by tasting the food, smelling the vegetation, and feeling the music and terrain over which the language has developed and flourished? Etymology - Word Origins

English is derived from several languages. If you spend a little time learning some of the roots of these other languages, high-level words in English will become very easy to remember. With a little bit of practice, you will be surprised how many new words you will understand without even using a dictionary. Finally, once you realize the interrelatedness of languages you will find you are well on your way to becoming a polyglot!
